Phil Welsh's Data Curation Exercise

    I chose datasets from the United States Geological Survey. Aside from being authoritative, trusted datasets, they are also generally free and without access constraints.

    This dataset contains over 400K points containing links to geochemical analyses of rock samples collected by the USGS. I believe this layer should be a part of this collection because geology generally deals with the earth's physical structure, and the rocks of which it is composed.

    Also, the dataset covers the whole world, so this collection would not be limited to American geologists.

  • Major Mineral Deposits of the World ðŸ”—

    • This USGS dataset portrays the locations of known deposits of major mineral commodities. Similar to the layer above, geologists study minerals for research, classification, and extraction. Ideally this dataset would help inform their decisions and research.

  • Quaternary Fault Database of the United States ðŸ”—

    • Depending on a geologist's area of research, they might require a dataset portraying fault lines. This dataset portrays faults and folds in the United States. The attribute information contains many fields including the name, age, slip rate, and a link to more information on the USGS website.

  • National Geochemical Database: Soil ðŸ”—

    • Along with rocks, tectonic plates, and water bodies, geologists are interested in soil. This USGS dataset shows detailed information about different soils and land cover in the United States.

    • This USGS dataset contains over 335K data points containing information regarding water samples' pH, sediment, color, and surrounding vegetation.
